And the Winner Is...

This weekend marks the 96th Academy Awards, and a few blockbusters are set to net multiple Oscars. Oppenheimer is up for the most awards (13), followed by Killers of the Flower Moon (11), Poor Things (11), and Barbie (eight). History may be made, as Lily Gladstone is the first Native American nominated for best actress; composer John Williams, at 91, is the event’s oldest nominee ever; and a documentary on Bobi Wine is the first Ugandan film to be nominated for an Oscar.

Spring Ahead

Don’t forget to set your clocks forward one hour this weekend as Daylight Saving Time (DST) occurs at 2:00 a.m. on Sunday. Sometimes called “summer time,” DST temporarily extends daylight hours during the time when most people are awake. But when did it start, and why?
Wartime roots
Although DST was first proposed by Benjamin Franklin in 1784, it began in the U.S. during World War I, primarily to save fuel by reducing the need to use artificial lighting. Some states continued to observe DST after the war was over, but it wasn’t officially observed again nationally until World War II.
Not for everyone
DST doesn’t exist in Arizona. The state is so hot and sunny that the additional hour of daylight meant energy consumption would soar, to keep living spaces cool for that extra hour. People were also unhappy waiting an hour longer to enjoy activities after sunset, which would happen at 9:00 p.m., making those activities fall late in the evening (a particular problem for people who had to work the next morning). So Arizona opted out of DST in 1967, and chose to remain on Standard Time. Arizona isn’t alone: Hawaii also ignores DST, but for a different reason. Hawaii’s proximity to the Equator means it doesn’t see much variation in daylight hours from season to season.
